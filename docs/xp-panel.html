3.1.3 Supplementary Demographics

Table 3.2 shows percentage of respondents in the woman+ category, median age, and the age distribution by inclusion group.

The observed variation in gender distribution between groups is notable. Of the full sample, a majority (58%) identified as women or preferred to self-identify. That skew is attenuated among youth respondents, of whom 70% did the same. Among social assistance recipients, it is inverted, with only 42% identifying as women or preferring to self-identify.

3.2 Labour Force Status

3.2.1 Employment, Enrollment

Table 3.3 summarizes the labour force status of respondents by inclusion group. Most respondents (54%) were employed. Between inclusion groups, newcomers were the most likely to be employed (57%), while those receiving social assistance were understandably the least likely (42%). Youth respondents were the most likely to be enrolled in education (57%), more than double the rate observed in the full sample (26%). Indigenous respondents reported conspicuously a conspicuously low rate of employment (44%) and a high rate of NEET status (44%).

3.2.2 Job Satisfaction

Table 3.4 summarizes net agreement1 with three statements concerning primary job2 satisfaction among employed respondents by inclusion group. We see evidence of higher overall job satisfaction among social assistance recipients and youth. Social assistance recipients are particularly interesting here, as they were both the most optimistic group with respect to advancement opportunities whilst also being the most concerned about job precarity. Indigenous respondents reported roughly average levels of overall satisfaction, but were by quite a wide margin the least optimistic about advancement opportunities.

3.3 Perspectives on Employment Services

3.3.1 Overview

Table 3.8 summarizes respondent awareness of, engagement with, and overall reflection on employment services by inclusion group. Awareness of employment services was lowest among youth respondents (67%) and newcomers (68%), and highest among social assistance recipients (93%) and Indigenous respondents (92%). Echoing our analysis of the client survey, we see that employment services has an overall reputation that could charitably be described as ‘mediocre’, and a reputation among Indigenous clients that cannot be charitably described at all. The only inclusion group with a positive NPS (+2) is social assistance reccipients, the same group with the highest rates of awareness and engagement.

Considering this from an outreach perspective, these results suggest the following:

  • The ‘market’ for employment services among social assistance recipients is saturated. An overwhelming majority of this group’s members are aware of and have engaged with employment services.
  • A similar majority of Indigenous respondents (92%) are aware of employment services. A smaller proportion, though still a majority (64%), have engaged with them. The implication here is that simply raising awareness of the existence of employment services won’t engage more Indigenous clients. Instead, a deeper reflection on and transformation of the approach to serving Indigenous clients is needed.
  • Respondents with disabilities follow a similar pattern, though with a somewhat lower level of awareness, and a much less acutely negative NPS.
  • Youth, newcomers, and racialized individuals represent the largest untapped ‘markets’ for employment services. Raising awareness within these groups is likely to result in increased engagement, though their uniformly negative NPS scores point to opportunities for improvement in the quality of services offered.

3.3.2 NPS among Clients

Table 3.9 shows the Net Promoter Score (NPS) for employment services among respondents who reported having used them. The NPS is calculated as the difference between the proportion of respondents who are promoters and the proportion who are detractors. Respondents were only shown the NPS question if they reported having used employment services, but the NPS calculation presented here includes all respondents, with those who did not use services coded as ‘Passive’ for the purposes of the calculation.

Show code 
d_sat <- d_long |>
    filter(es_client) |>
    mutate(
        es_promoter = es_nps_group %in% "Promoter",
        es_detractor = es_nps_group %in% "Detractor",
        es_nps_marginal = 100 * (es_promoter - es_detractor),
    ) |>
    arrange(
        ig
    ) |>
    mutate(
        ig = fct_inorder(ig)
    )

t_es_nps <- d_sat |>
    group_by(ig = inclusion_group_label) |>
    summarize(
        nps = mean(es_nps_marginal),
        promoter = mean(es_promoter),
        detractor = mean(es_detractor),
    )


t_es_nps |>
    ungroup() |>
    gt() |>
    gtExtras::gt_theme_538() |>
    gtExtras::gt_color_box(
        columns = c(promoter, detractor),
        domain = c(0, 1),
        scale = 100,
        suffix = "%",
        palette = c("#0000FF", "#f2f2f2", "#ff4500")
    ) |>
    tab_style(
        style = cell_fill(color = "gray95"),
        locations = cells_body(
            columns = nps
        )
    ) |>
    fmt_number(columns = nps, decimals = 0) |>
    tab_footnote(
        "NPS is calculated as the difference between the proportion of respondents who are promoters and the proportion who are detractors. Here, we calculate NPS among respondents who reported having used employment services. In this table, non-client respondents were removed from the denominator"
